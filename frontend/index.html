what we did
The setup

We ran 99 of your TikToks through TRIBE v2 — a model from Meta FAIR that predicts, second-by-second, which parts of a viewer's brain activate while watching a video. It's trained on thousands of hours of brain scans (fMRI) paired with video stimuli.

Then we compared the brain predictions against your real per-second audience-retention data and asked: what's different in videos where people stay vs videos where they leave after the first few seconds?

the outlier worth studying
High brain score, low retention: what's going on?

Video 7556715516782497037 scores extremely high on both face (0.48) and motion (0.49) — by the model's logic, the content itself is deeply engaging. But only 6% of viewers made it to the end.

This is a signal that the hook in the first 1–2 seconds isn't matching the brain-engagement the rest of the video could deliver. The model doesn't score the first impression — it scores the stimulus content. If viewers leave before they see it, the brain score doesn't help. what doesn't predict retention
🧠
≈ 0
Mind-wandering (DMN)
The default-mode network — associated with mind-wandering — shows essentially zero difference between high and low retention. Losing viewers isn't about boring them into daydreaming. It's about failing to engage the right circuits fast enough.
🌍
≈ 0
Scene recognition (PPA)
The place/scene area shows no gap. Your backdrop, your room, your environment — viewers don't care. What's in motion and who's speaking matters; where you're standing doesn't.
what to do with this
1
Put your face in the first frame, every time
The face area (FFA) is the single strongest predictor. If your thumbnail or first 0.5s shows a screen, text, or keyboard — you're burning the biggest engagement signal before it can fire. Open with your face.
2
Demo and talk simultaneously
The strongest videos hit face + motion + speech at the same time. Talking to camera while showing a screen demo, typing while narrating, or reacting while demonstrating — this combo keeps three brain systems firing in parallel.
3
Match what you're saying to what's on screen
The STS (speech-processing) gap is the biggest in relative terms (+80%). This area responds when audio and visual information make sense together. Cut aways, stock footage, or unrelated b-roll while you narrate will drop this signal.
4
Don't decorate, demonstrate
Scene recognition (PPA) is flat — your background doesn't help. Motion detection (MT+) is the second-strongest signal. Hands moving, terminals scrolling, cursor moving, face gesturing — kinetic content keeps the visual system active.

methodology & caveats
What this is and isn't

TRIBE v2 predicts the average brain's response to a video stimulus — it's an encoding model trained on 1,117 hours of fMRI data across 25 subjects. It's not measuring your specific viewers' brains; it's predicting what a typical human brain would do watching this content.

These results are from 99 short videos (4–13s), the first pass of a full 696-video sweep that's still running. Short videos over-represent completion since there's less time to drop off. The per-second drop-off analysis (correlating exactly which brain region is active the moment someone leaves) will be available once longer video processing finishes.

The model uses a 5-second hemodynamic lag — brain responses peak ~5s after the stimulus. Scores reflect the content quality of the video, not the hook in the first 0.5s. That's why the outlier above is instructive.
